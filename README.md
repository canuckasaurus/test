1. Wallet & Key Management (Custody Operations)
Security is the primary mandate for a bank's crypto operations. Even with Multi-Party Computation (MPC) or smart contract wallets, human governance is required.

[ ] Multi-Sig / Quorum Approvals: Acting as human "signers" for high-value transactions or policy changes, requiring coordinated approval from multiple executives/operators (the Maker-Checker process).

[ ] Address Whitelisting/Blacklisting: Manually verifying and approving new external deposit/withdrawal addresses before they are added to the institution's automated systems.

[ ] Liquidity Rebalancing (Hot/Warm/Cold): Monitoring thresholds and manually initiating transfers of excess funds from vulnerable "hot" (online) wallets to secure "cold" (offline) storage, or vice versa, to meet daily liquidity needs.

[ ] Disaster Recovery Ceremonies: Participating in routine drills to recover wallet backups, hardware security modules (HSMs), or seed phrases from physical vaults to ensure business continuity.

2. Transaction Settlement & Transfers
While small customer withdrawals are automated, large settlements and exceptions fall to the back office.

[ ] Manual OTC Settlement: Coordinating Over-The-Counter (OTC) block trades. This involves verifying receipt of fiat via SWIFT/FedWire and manually releasing the corresponding crypto assets.

[ ] Gas & Fee Management: Monitoring native gas tokens (e.g., ETH, SOL, MATIC) across operating wallets and manually topping up fee wallets to ensure automated transactions don't stall.

[ ] Exception Handling (Stuck Transactions): Identifying transactions stuck in the mempool due to network congestion or low gas fees, and manually executing "Speed Up" (Replace-by-Fee) or "Cancel" functions.

[ ] Cross-Chain Bridging: Approving and monitoring the manual transfer of assets across different blockchain networks (e.g., moving USDC from Ethereum to Polygon), which carries high smart contract risk.

3. Token Lifecycle (Minting & Burning)
If the bank is issuing its own tokenized assets (like a bank-backed stablecoin or tokenized Treasury bills), the back office controls the supply.

[ ] Fiat Verification (Pre-Mint): Manually reconciling the traditional banking system to confirm that fiat funds have successfully settled in the reserve bank account.

[ ] Token Minting: Initiating the smart contract function to mint the exact corresponding amount of tokens on-chain, requiring a multi-sig approval process.

[ ] Redemption Processing (Pre-Burn): Receiving customer requests to redeem tokens for fiat, verifying the customer's KYC status, and validating the token return.

[ ] Token Burning: Executing the smart contract function to permanently destroy the returned tokens.

[ ] Fiat Disbursement: Instructing the traditional wire team to release the equivalent fiat funds back to the customer's bank account.

4. Staking & Corporate Actions
If the bank offers yield or holds Proof-of-Stake (PoS) assets.

[ ] Validator Provisioning: Manually initiating the delegation of institutional assets to specific, vetted validator nodes.

[ ] Unstaking & Lockup Management: Managing the queue of unstaking requests, tracking cool-down periods (which can take days or weeks depending on the protocol), and ensuring liquidity is available for clients.

[ ] Reward Claiming: Manually triggering smart contracts to harvest staking yields or liquidity pool rewards on a set schedule.

[ ] Airdrop/Fork Management: Evaluating network hard forks or airdrops, deciding whether to support the new asset, and manually claiming/distributing the new tokens if supported.

5. Reconciliation & Treasury
Crypto moves 24/7, while traditional banking has cut-off times, creating a massive reconciliation burden.

[ ] On-Chain vs. Internal Ledger Recon: Manually investigating discrepancies between the bank's internal database (customer balances) and the actual on-chain wallet balances.

[ ] Fiat-to-Crypto Recon: Ensuring that every dollar of fiat sitting in a stablecoin reserve account perfectly matches the circulating supply of minted tokens on the blockchain.

[ ] Dust Management: Periodically sweeping "dust" (tiny, remnant fractions of tokens left in wallets) into a central treasury wallet to maintain clean ledgers.

6. Compliance, Risk, & Audit
Regulatory scrutiny for banks handling crypto is intense, requiring heavy manual oversight.

[ ] Transaction Monitoring Alerts: Reviewing flagged transactions generated by blockchain analytics tools (like Chainalysis, Elliptic, or TRM Labs) for potential Anti-Money Laundering (AML) or sanctions violations.

[ ] Travel Rule Compliance: Manually intervening when automated Travel Rule protocols (like VerifyVASP) fail, requiring operators to directly message counterparties to exchange originator/beneficiary information before releasing a transfer.

[ ] Proof of Reserves Reporting: Compiling cryptographic proofs and internal ledger reports for third-party auditors to verify that customer assets are fully backed 1:1.

Estimated Headcount by Operational Activity
Activity Category	Min. People Per Shift	Total Headcount (24/7 Coverage)	Resourcing Rationale
1. Wallet & Key Management	2	8 – 10	Security protocols dictate that no single person can move funds. You need at least two operators awake and active per shift to serve as multi-sig approvers for high-value transfers, whitelisting, and liquidity rebalancing.
2. Transaction Settlement & Transfers	2	8 – 10	This team actively monitors gas, processes stuck transactions, and settles large OTC blocks. At scale, exception handling alone requires constant attention. This team often overlaps with the Wallet Management signers.
3. Token Lifecycle (Minting & Burning)	2 (Business Hours)	3 – 4	Unlike general transfers, minting and burning stablecoins or tokenized assets doesn't necessarily run 24/7. It usually aligns with traditional banking hours to ensure the underlying fiat reserve wires can be verified and settled.
4. Staking & Corporate Actions	1 (Business Hours)	1 – 2	Managing validators, unstaking queues, and claiming rewards is generally handled by senior analysts as a daily or weekly batch process rather than a continuous, around-the-clock shift operation.
5. Reconciliation & Treasury	1 – 2	4 – 6	While traditional banking relies on end-of-day cut-offs, crypto requires continuous fiat-to-crypto and internal-to-on-chain reconciliation to catch accounting drift or dust accumulation before it impacts trading balances.
6. Compliance, Risk, & Audit	2	8 – 10	This scales linearly with transaction volume. Intercepting Travel Rule failures and reviewing Chainalysis/TRM alerts for sanctions violations requires active, 24/7 human oversight to prevent frozen funds or regulatory breaches.
The Shared Services Overlap
For a mid-to-large operation, you are realistically looking at a dedicated back-office headcount of 15 to 25 people covering the globe. This accounts for weekend coverage, holidays, and the stringent security requirement that multiple people must be online simultaneously to execute the maker-checker workflows.
